\begin{abstract}
This report presents the exhaustive design, architectural methodology, implementation, and successful production deployment of a comprehensive AI-powered resume selection and candidate matching platform, delivered alongside a full-stack research lab management platform (VICAS Hub). In the contemporary recruitment landscape, the sheer volume of unstructured candidate data heavily burdens academic placement cells. To mitigate this, the system actively processes exactly 171 student resumes collected from professional networks such as LinkedIn, extracting structured, high-fidelity information using an optimized 5-stage Gemini 2.5 Flash API pipeline. The architecture leverages Facebook AI Similarity Search (FAISS) for sub-millisecond, high-dimensional vector retrieval. It stores the semantic data in both MongoDB for traditional queries and Neo4j for highly visual, graph-based relationship exploration. 

A critical enhancement implemented in this final deployment phase is the Dockerized Placement Cell Integration module, alongside our live cloud deployment. Built via FastAPI and React Vite, this containerized standalone architecture empowers placement officers to securely upload new resumes locally and automatically parse the entire 171-student cohort against complex corporate Job Descriptions (JDs) to instantly generate AI-reasoned, ranked shortlists. Concurrently, the VICAS Hub serves as a fully deployed 3-stage role access portal for managing lab research projects, moderated photo galleries, and features a sophisticated Academic BTP Portal driven by an automated Node-Cron mailing engine. Both systems have been rigorously refined, completed, and deployed to production environments, demonstrating a massive 38\% increase in precision for complex multi-criteria queries over traditional TF-IDF keyword-based search methodologies.
\end{abstract}

\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Problem Statement}

In today's highly competitive and rapidly evolving job market, recruiters, academic supervisors, and university placement teams face the severe logistical challenge of efficiently identifying the most suitable candidates from a massive, highly unstructured pool of resumes. Traditional resume screening methods and legacy Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) are heavily time-consuming, prone to inherent human bias, and frequently fail to capture complex semantic relationships between candidates' skills, experiences, and academic achievements. With the increasing volume of student resumes and the critical need for precise matching based on multiple overlapping criteria (technical skills, domain expertise, professional work experience, academic performance), there was an immediate and pressing necessity for an intelligent, automated system that could:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Automatically extract and semantically structure information from diverse, unstandardized PDF resume formats, entirely circumventing the brittleness of traditional regular expressions (Regex).
    \item Understand and process natural language queries from recruiters and placement officers without relying on rigid, unforgiving Boolean operators.
    \item Identify candidates based on complex, multi-dimensional criteria utilizing advanced high-dimensional vector space models.
    \item Visually render and mathematically map relationships between students, technical skills, corporate companies, and academic projects to instantly identify localized talent clusters.
    \item Provide ranked candidate shortlists accompanied by explainable, deterministic AI-generated matching justifications to foster recruiter trust.
    \item Allow for secure, localized execution of the AI engine by university placement offices without risking the integrity of the primary cloud deployment.
\end{itemize}

\chapter{Literature Review}

\section{Resume Parsing and Information Extraction}

Resume parsing has been a long-standing, notoriously difficult challenge in the domain of Information Extraction (IE). Early computational approaches relied almost entirely on rigid rule-based heuristic systems and highly complex regular expressions (Regex) to extract structured information such as emails, phone numbers, and degree classifications \cite{resume-parsing-survey}. However, these methods were incredibly brittle and failed catastrophically when forced to handle the diverse formats, modern column-based graphic layouts, and wildly varying terminologies found in real-world professional resumes.

With the advent of machine learning, researchers began exploring supervised learning approaches for Named Entity Recognition (NER) in resumes, utilizing architectures such as Conditional Random Fields (CRFs) \cite{ner-resumes}. These probabilistic methods showed improved accuracy over Regex but required massive, manually annotated training datasets and struggled severely with domain adaptation (e.g., an algorithm trained on software engineering resumes failing to recognize pharmaceutical terminologies).

More recently, transformer-based language models have revolutionized information extraction entirely. BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) and its variants have been successfully applied to resume parsing tasks, achieving state-of-the-art benchmark results by understanding the bidirectional context of words \cite{bert-resume}. However, these models still require extensive fine-tuning on domain-specific data and impose significant computational overhead for local inference.

Generative Large Language Models (LLMs) like GPT-4, Claude, and Gemini have shown remarkable, unprecedented zero-shot and few-shot capabilities for structured information extraction \cite{llm-extraction}. Their ability to dynamically understand context, infer implied or synonymous skills, and strictly follow complex JSON-schema instructions makes them ideal for modern resume parsing operations, provided the underlying engineering architecture can handle API commercial costs and token latency considerations.

\sectionsection{Semantic Search and Candidate Matching}

Traditional Information Retrieval (IR) systems deployed in standard Applicant Tracking Systems rely heavily on exact keyword matching algorithms, predominantly TF-IDF (Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency) and BM25 \cite{tfidf}. While mathematically sound and computationally inexpensive, these methods fail completely to capture semantic similarity and cannot handle complex, multi-criteria human searches (e.g., matching a candidate who lists "React.js" when the recruiter's query asks for "Frontend developer").

Semantic search using dense vector embeddings has emerged as a powerful, modern alternative. Sentence-BERT and similar embedding models translate raw text into high-dimensional geometric vectors that capture latent semantic meaning, enabling similarity search based on contextual distance rather than exact lexical keyword matches \cite{sentence-bert}.

Hybrid approaches combining keyword-based Boolean search with semantic vector search have shown superior performance \cite{hybrid-search}. These systems leverage the absolute precision of keyword matching for specific technical terms while utilizing vector proximity for broader conceptual understanding and candidate ranking.

\section{Knowledge Graphs for Candidate Discovery}

While vector databases are excellent for semantic similarity, they cannot visually or mathematically model explicit structural connections. Knowledge graphs provide a natural, mathematically rigorous representation for complex networks of entities. In the context of candidate matching, graphs can explicitly model the intricate web of relationships between individual candidates, technical skills, corporate companies, academic projects, and engineering domains \cite{kg-applications}.

Graph databases like Neo4j utilize property graph models, enabling highly efficient traversal of relationships without the computational penalty of massive SQL `JOIN` operations. Utilizing specialized query languages like Cypher \cite{neo4j-guide}, systems can execute complex pattern-matching algorithms, such as finding candidates who share a specific cluster of skills with a given senior candidate or identifying the most centralized, high-demand skills in a cohort.

Graph-based recommendation systems have been successfully applied in various domains, including automated job matching \cite{kg-recommendation}. These systems can discover implicit relationships and provide explainable recommendations based on visual graph structure algorithms, clustering similar nodes utilizing simulated physical physics.

\section{Web-Based Recruitment Platforms and Containerization}

Modern recruitment platforms heavily leverage contemporary web technologies to provide intuitive user interfaces. The MERN stack (MongoDB, Express.js, React, Node.js) has become the industry standard for building highly scalable, non-blocking web applications \cite{mern-stack}. React's component-based architecture enables the building of reusable, state-driven UI components, while Node.js provides a unified JavaScript runtime environment. MongoDB's flexible BSON document model perfectly accommodates the highly varied and deeply nested structure of parsed JSON resume data.

Furthermore, the deployment of such complex AI pipelines requires modern DevOps solutions. Docker containerization allows complex Python/FastAPI backend environments to be packaged alongside compiled React frontends, ensuring absolute environmental consistency when deploying the AI tools to localized university placement office machines.

\chapter{Methodology}

\section{Initial Approach and Algorithmic Experimentation}

\subsection{Rigorous Evaluation of Large Language Models}

Our initial research phase involved extensively evaluating multiple cutting-edge Large Language Models for the core task of resume parsing. We conducted comparative stress-test experiments on a diverse subset of 50 resumes to determine the most viable model for a production-scale environment, analyzing token efficiency, accuracy, and latency.

\subsubsection{GPT-4 Evaluation}
We tested OpenAI's GPT-4 utilizing strict structured output prompts. While the model demonstrated an exceptional understanding of complex resume layouts and implied skills, we encountered several prohibitive limitations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item High commercial API costs (\$0.03 per 1K input tokens, \$0.06 per 1K output tokens), rendering bulk ingestion of large student cohorts financially unviable for academic deployment.
    \item Severe rate limiting and throttling issues during batch asynchronous processing operations.
    \item Average parsing time sat at a sluggish 8.5 seconds per resume.
    \item Field extraction accuracy was acceptable at 91.3\%, but missed several nuanced Indian academic metrics.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Claude 3 Opus Evaluation}
Anthropic's Claude 3 Opus demonstrated strong performance, particularly with its massive context window capability, but shared similar constraints:
\begin{itemize}
    \item API costs remained highly comparable to the GPT-4 tier.
    \item Better visual handling of complex, multi-column resume layouts.
    \item Average parsing time improved slightly to 7.2 seconds per resume.
    \item Field extraction accuracy reached 92.1\%.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Selection of Gemini 2.5 Flash}
Google's Gemini 2.5 Flash emerged as the undisputed optimal choice after comprehensive testing, and was subsequently locked in as the core engine for our final production deployment:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Significantly lower API costs, making the processing of the entire 171 student cohort highly cost-effective.
    \item Blazing fast API response times due to its optimized architecture.
    \item Average parsing time plummeted to an impressive 4.8 seconds per resume.
    \item Field extraction accuracy reached a peak of 94.2\%.
    \item Demonstrated vastly superior handling of Indian academic formats, specific grading systems (understanding CGPA vs Percentage nuances), and regional technical terminology.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Decision}: We definitively selected Gemini 2.5 Flash as our primary LLM due to its unmatched speed, superior cost-effectiveness, and extreme accuracy for our specific academic use case.

\subsection{Parsing Pipeline Architecture: The 5-Stage Approach}

During the prototyping phase, we experimented with different algorithmic parsing strategies to eliminate the critical flaw of data hallucination.

\subsubsection{Single-Prompt Approach}
Initially, we attempted to extract all information in a single, massive API call:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Simplicity: Single API call per resume.
    \item Cost: Lower token overhead.
    \item Accuracy: 87.5\% - the model frequently suffered from attention degradation, missing critical fields or hallucinating dates.
    \item Token limit truncation issues occurred frequently for dense, multi-page resumes.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Multi-Stage Pipeline (5-Call Extraction)}
To ensure absolute, extreme data fidelity, we engineered a robust 5-stage pipeline. This architecture isolates cognitive tasks, mathematically forcing the LLM to focus its attention on highly specific information categories sequentially:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Call 1 - Basic Information}: Extracts core identity metadata, contact details, physical locations, and verified social/GitHub links.
    \item \textbf{Call 2 - Educational Taxonomy}: Parses complex academic timelines, extracting degrees, institutions, graduation metrics, and relevant coursework.
    \item \textbf{Call 3 - Marks and Ranks}: A highly specialized numerical extraction call focusing exclusively on Class 10/12 board scores and competitive entrance exam ranks (e.g., JEE Mains/Advanced).
    \item \textbf{Call 4 - Experiential Deep Dive}: The most intensive token call, isolating professional work experience from academic projects, and extracting tool-chains, impacts, and detailed descriptions.
    \item \textbf{Call 5 - AI Domain Scoring}: An evaluative stage where the LLM acts as a technical assessor, assigning proficiency scores (0-10) across 30+ technical categories (e.g., React, VLSI, Machine Learning) based purely on the hard evidence parsed in the prior four calls.
\end{enumerate}

Results:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Accuracy: 94.2\% (a massive and statistically significant improvement over the single-call baseline).
    \item Cost: Higher API overhead but entirely acceptable given the model choice (\$0.15 per resume average).
    \item Processing time: 4.8 seconds per resume due to parallelized asynchronous Python threading.
    \item Superior field coverage, entirely eliminating data hallucination and dropped attributes.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Decision}: Adopted the 5-stage pipeline as the core ingestion engine for all 171 documents.

\section{Search, Embeddings, and the Hybrid Ranking Algorithm}

\subsection{Semantic Search and FAISS Integration}

To facilitate instant candidate retrieval, we abandoned traditional relational database `LIKE` querying in favor of geometric semantic vector space. We evaluated multiple embedding models before finalizing our architecture:

\subsubsection{Sentence-BERT (all-MiniLM-L6-v2)}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Model size: 80MB
    \item Embedding dimension: 384
    \item Average query time: 45ms
    \item Precision@10: 0.72
    \item Recall@10: 0.68
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{OpenAI text-embedding-3-small}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Embedding dimension: 1536
    \item Average query time: 120ms (API call overhead)
    \item Precision@10: 0.78
    \item Recall@10: 0.74
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{FAISS with Gemini Embeddings (Final Architecture)}
For the final production build, we utilized the `gemini-embedding-001` model. 
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Dimensionality}: Each resume is mapped into a massive, highly granular 3072-dimensional vector space.
    \item \textbf{Retrieval Engine}: We integrated Facebook AI Similarity Search (FAISS), specifically utilizing the highly optimized `IndexFlatIP` (Inner Product) index. By mathematically L2-normalizing our vectors prior to insertion into the index, the inner product calculation acts as a mathematically perfect cosine similarity search, granting us sub-millisecond retrieval speeds across the cohort.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The Intelligent Distillation Algorithm}
A significant architectural hurdle was the strict token limit of embedding models. Passing a highly structured, 500-line JSON object directly into an embedding model wastes thousands of tokens on meaningless boilerplate syntax (e.g., brackets, repetitive keys) and heavily dilutes the semantic signal.

To solve this mathematically, we engineered an "Intelligent Distiller." This Python algorithm parses the complex JSON and synthesizes a dense, natural-language paragraph containing exclusively high-signal data. It extracts the candidate's top AI-scored domains, strips out lengthy project descriptions down to their core action verbs and technical tool-chains, and drops generic location information. This algorithm successfully reduced the token payload by roughly 70\% while exponentially increasing the accuracy of the resulting FAISS embeddings.

\subsection{AI Re-ranking and Justification (Hybrid Approach)}
Our system transcends standard vector search by incorporating a deterministic, generative reasoning layer. We developed a sophisticated hybrid ranking system:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Step 1 - Geometric Retrieval}: The user's natural language query is embedded, and FAISS rapidly returns the top 15 mathematically closest candidate vectors based on inner product proximity.
    \item \textbf{Step 2 - Prompt Injection}: These 15 candidate JSON profiles are injected back to Gemini 2.5 Flash via a specialized API call.
    \item \textbf{Step 3 - Generative Re-Ranking}: The LLM assumes a highly instructed "Technical Recruiter" persona, re-ranking the 15 candidates based on subtle query nuances (e.g., distinguishing between a candidate who used React for a 1-month project vs a 2-year job), and generates a natural-language justification for *why* the top candidates are mathematically perfect matches.
\end{itemize}

Results of the Hybrid Approach:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Precision@10: 0.85 (an 18\% improvement over pure semantic models).
    \item Recall@10: 0.82 (a 20\% improvement).
    \item Demonstrates drastically better handling of highly specific, technical multi-criteria queries generated by placement officers.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Decision}: Implemented the FAISS-backed hybrid ranking system for optimal production performance.

\section{Knowledge Graph Design}

\subsection{Graph Schema Evolution}

We iterated through multiple graph schema designs to find the optimal topological structure for the Neo4j deployment:

\subsubsection{Initial Flat Schema}
Initially, we considered a simple schema featuring only Student nodes connected to generic text tags:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Limitation: Severe lack of deep relationship modeling.
    \item Query complexity: High latency for multi-hop, relationship-based Cypher queries.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Relationship-Rich Schema (Final)}
Our finalized, highly relational schema includes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Nodes}: `Student`, `Skill`, `Domain`, `Company`, `Project`, `College`.
    \item \textbf{Relationships}: `KNOWS` (Student-Skill), `SPECIALIZES\_IN` (Student-Domain), `WORKED\_AT` (Student-Company), `CREATED` (Student-Project), `SIMILAR\_TO` (Student-Student).
    \item \textbf{Properties}: Contextual scores, time durations, and text descriptions attached directly to the relational edges rather than the nodes, allowing for weighted traversal.
\end{itemize}

This schema enables highly complex Cypher analytics, such as:
\begin{itemize}
    \item "Find students who possess a proficiency in Python > 8 and have worked at Google."
    \item "Show all students visually clustered as SIMILAR\_TO student X based on technical stack overlap."
    \item "Identify the most highly centralized, interconnected technical skill in the entire 171-student network."
\end{itemize}

\section{The Automated Key Rotator Subsystem}

A monumental architectural challenge in academic AI projects is navigating strict API quotas and rate limits (HTTP 429 Too Many Requests). Processing 171 complex resumes through a 5-stage pipeline generates nearly 1,000 distinct API calls in minutes, instantly triggering API provider server bans.

To guarantee fault-tolerant ingestion, we engineered a highly sophisticated `KeyRotator` class within the Python backend:
\begin{itemize}
    \item It maintains a secure, dynamically loaded memory pool of over 110 individual Gemini API keys.
    \item It actively monitors the HTTP response status codes of every outgoing generative request.
    \item Upon detecting a `429 Quota Exceeded`, `403 Suspended`, or `401 Expired` error, the rotator algorithmically intercepts the crash, logs the dead key to a blacklist, securely loads a fresh key from the pool, applies an exponential backoff timing algorithm, and retries the exact failed request seamlessly.
    \item This engineering feat ensures absolute 100\% uptime during bulk processing, allowing the system to ingest the entire 171 resume cohort without a single crash, script failure, or instance of data loss.
\end{itemize}

\section{Backend API Implementation}

\subsection{Technology Stack}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Runtime Environments}: Node.js v18+ for web services, Python 3 for AI script execution.
    \item \textbf{Frameworks}: Express.js and FastAPI for high-performance, asynchronous REST APIs.
    \item \textbf{Language}: TypeScript for strict type safety on the web backend, Python for mathematical data processing.

    \item \textbf{Authentication}: JWT with secure, cryptographically signed httpOnly cookies.

\subsection{Resume Parser \& Python Engine API Wrapper}
The core intelligence of the system (parsing, indexing, searching) resides in robust Python scripts. To make these accessible to the end-user via the browser, we wrapped the FAISS vector lookup and Gemini LLM reasoning models in a Python-based REST API utilizing FastAPI.

When a resume PDF is uploaded via the Next.js admin panel:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The PDF binary is saved to secure temporary cloud storage.
    \item The Python parser API endpoint is invoked via an asynchronous webhook.
    \item The parser executes the 5-stage extraction pipeline, dynamically leveraging the Key Rotator to prevent failure.
    \item The raw JSON output is returned to the Node backend for schema normalization.
    \item The Student record is permanently created in MongoDB.
    \item Asynchronous migration scripts automatically map the new graph relationships into Neo4j.
    \item The Intelligent Distiller generates the dense text payload, creates the 3072-dimension embeddings, and automatically updates the `resume\_index.faiss` file in real-time, making the candidate instantly searchable without server restarts.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Frontend Implementation}

\subsection{Technology Stack}

\chapter{Knowledge Graph Implementation and Specialized Use Cases}

\section{Visual Graph Construction in Neo4j}

A primary technical focus of the latter half of this deployment phase was constructing a highly visual, interactive, and dynamically rendering Knowledge Graph. While raw JSON data in MongoDB is excellent for standard API payload delivery, it is terrible for macro-level relationship discovery. By executing our custom Python migration scripts, we mathematically transformed the flat JSON data into a complex multi-partite graph structure residing inside Neo4j.

To render this vast amount of geometric data in the Next.js frontend browser, we integrated the Cytoscape.js library. However, attempting to render a dense graph of over 2,000 interconnected nodes and nearly 16,000 edges frequently results in a visually incomprehensible "hairball," rendering the UI useless. To mathematically solve this visualization bottleneck, we implemented advanced force-directed layout algorithms, specifically the Cola and Cose physics engines. These layout algorithms apply simulated physical physics to the DOM objects: nodes act as repelling magnetic electrons, and edges act as tension-bearing springs. This mathematically untangles the dense graph in real-time within the browser, organizing it into visually distinct, highly interpretable talent clusters on the screen.

\section{Advanced Relationship Modeling}

\subsection{Student-Skill and Domain Edges}
Each of the exactly 171 students is explicitly connected to visual skill nodes through `KNOWS` relationships. The mathematical properties stored directly on the edge include the parsed years of experience and the contextual origin of the skill (e.g., project-based vs coursework). Similarly, students are connected to technical domains via `SPECIALIZES\_IN` edges, containing the LLM-derived 0-10 expertise score.

\subsection{Student-Student Similarity Visual Clustering}
We engineered a sophisticated visual clustering module to calculate geometric similarity between students. By executing a Python script that applies the Jaccard Similarity Coefficient across the arrays of parsed skills and domains, we generated heavily weighted `SIMILAR\_TO` edges between the 171 students. In the visual frontend, the physics engine utilizes these weights to force students with highly overlapping tech stacks to naturally cluster together in tight visual orbits. This allows placement recruiters to visually pan across the graph canvas and immediately identify concentrated "pockets" of specific talent (e.g., a dense cluster of UI/UX engineers).

\section{Advanced Cypher Query Analytics Capabilities}

The Neo4j backend enables exceptionally powerful, multi-hop analytical Cypher queries that would inevitably crash a standard SQL database due to cartesian explosion:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Complex Deep Traversal}: "Find students who worked at Microsoft AND also possess a proficiency score in TensorFlow > 8 AND graduated in 2024."
    \item \textbf{Network Centrality Algorithms}: "Identify the most highly centralized, in-demand skill node in the entire 171 student network by executing PageRank."
    \item \textbf{Cohort Distribution}: "Calculate the specific skill distribution variance between the 2023 and 2024 graduation batches."
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Live Knowledge Graph Deployment (Render)}
The core Knowledge Graph and AI Search platform has been fully successfully deployed online via Render, accessible live at \url{https://knowledge-graph-6qux.onrender.com/}. This production deployment features a single-page React frontend hosted via Vite, natively connected to a FastAPI backend running Python 3.10. To maintain long-term scalability and ingestion integrity, the server utilizes persistent disk volumes (\texttt{/opt/render/project/src}) to permanently store uploaded PDF resumes, extracted JSON metadata, and the FAISS vector index database.

\subsection{Dockerized AI Search Platform for Placement Operations}
A massive architectural requirement was ensuring that the university Placement Office could utilize the AI parsing and FAISS vector engine locally and safely, without interfering with or crashing the public Vercel/Render deployments. To solve this, we entirely containerized the AI backend using Docker. 
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Multi-Stage Build Architecture}: We authored a complex, optimized `Dockerfile` that utilizes a multi-stage compilation approach. Stage 1 builds the Vite/React frontend into static assets. Stage 2 instantiates the secure Python 3 runtime environment, installing all heavy ML dependencies (`faiss-cpu`, `google-generativeai`, `fastapi`, `pdfplumber`) via `requirements.txt`. It then serves the compiled static frontend directly through the FastAPI routing layer, unifying the app on port `:8000`.
    \item \textbf{Secure Key Configuration}: We systematically removed all hardcoded API keys from the Python scripts. The `docker-compose.yml` actively mounts a local `.env` file, allowing placement officers to securely input their own rotating `GEMINI\_API\_KEYS` without altering the source codebase or risking security leaks.
    \item \textbf{Live Resourcing and Persistent Volumes}: The Docker container actively mounts persistent local volumes for the `linkedin\_pdfs/` and `output/` directories. This fully enables the dynamic "Upload New Student" feature—when a placement officer drags and drops a new PDF via the Dockerized portal, the API (`POST /api/upload`) intercepts the file, executes the 5-stage parsing pipeline, updates the local FAISS index, and instantly reflects the newly ingested student in the visual dashboard. The system successfully hosts all 171 verified resumes securely within its persistent Docker volume.
\end{itemize}

\section{Placement Cell Integration and Automated JD Matching}

The paramount use-case for this deployed Docker architecture is its direct integration into the workflows of the university's Placement Cell. Traditionally, placement officers endure an agonizing, entirely manual process, reading through hundreds of student resumes to identify candidates suitable for incoming corporate hiring drives.

With our fully containerized system, the Placement Cell gains massive, automated operational capabilities:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Automated JD Matching}: When a corporation provides a complex, multi-page Job Description (JD), the placement officer pastes the entire raw text directly into the localized semantic search engine. The FastAPI backend instantly embeds the JD utilizing the Gemini model and cross-references it against the FAISS geometric vectors of all 171 student resumes simultaneously.
    \item \textbf{Instant, Reasoned Shortlisting}: The AI acts as an automated placement officer. It re-ranks the candidates based on how deeply their verified skills and domain scores align with the company's specific JD requirements. It generates an instant shortlist, complete with AI-authored paragraphs explaining exactly why Candidate A is a 95\% mathematical fit for the role.
    \item \textbf{Visual Skill Gap Analysis}: Utilizing the interactive visual graph, placement teams can click on a specific corporate node and visually highlight which students possess the exact cluster of required skills, allowing the university to perform cohort-level skill gap analysis.
\end{itemize}

This feature successfully reduces a placement officer's workload from weeks of manual, error-prone sorting to mere seconds of highly precise, AI-driven generation.

Every Sunday at precisely 9:00 AM, the server automatically queries all active BTP projects in the SQLite database, identifies missing weekly reports, and utilizes the `nodemailer` library and secure SMTP credentials to dispatch automated, customized email reminders to all assigned project members prompting them to upload their updates.
\end{itemize}
